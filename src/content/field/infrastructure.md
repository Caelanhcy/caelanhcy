<span class="field-lead">Infrastructure</span>. A boring word for the thing that decides whether a company is alive in five years. Try a question that sounds slightly silly and then take it completely seriously: how does a team know it has succeeded? Not in the abstract, not in the all-hands. On a Tuesday, at the end of a launch, when nobody is in the mood for ceremony — what is the number, who reads it first, what happens next. Most teams cannot answer cleanly. They wrote the brief and forgot to write the finish line. A function without a defined success metric is a treadmill that mistakes effort for progress. Infrastructure is what stops the running.

## The Thesis

Infrastructure is not bureaucracy. It is the load-bearing structure that lets a team operate at scale without re-deciding every question. A function is rules made into a team. A team is rules made into a calendar. A calendar is rules made into a quarter that compounds.

The deeper discipline is measurement. You cannot improve a function whose definition of success you have not written down.

## The Misconception

The default reading of infrastructure is administrative overhead. Process slows the team. Rules constrain creativity. The fastest companies have the fewest rules.

The fastest companies have the *cleanest* rules — not the fewest. The myth of the lean startup with no process is mostly a survival-bias story; the ones that lived past Series A all built rules quickly, they just built the right ones. What kills speed is unclear rules. What kills clarity is rules without measurement. The work is to make the rules legible and the measurement load-bearing, so that the team stops re-deciding the same question every week.

## The Actual Discipline

Two principles carry everything else. They are not negotiable. They come in order.

**Principle one — know what success looks like before you start.** Not "what do we want to achieve" in the abstract. The specific, measurable outcome that proves the work succeeded. If you cannot write it down before you begin, you do not have a function. You have an activity. The CR50 program at DIER (the top 50 SKUs rebranded and consolidated for Black Friday) worked because the success metric was written before the campaign — channel traffic to lift 10–15% YoY, flash-sale pages to contribute 15–20% of organic traffic during the window. Both happened. The Q4 2024 headline outcome was 153% YoY Black Friday growth on the flagship brand. The number is not the point. The point is that the number existed as a goal before the program started.

**Principle two — wire the measurement before you build the function.** The temptation when nothing exists is to start hiring. The discipline is to start measuring. The DIER research-and-analytics function was built from scratch — the organisation had no formal research, analytics, or trend infrastructure when the work began. Department-wide data governance came first. Naming. Storing. Reporting. Cross-functional visibility. Only after the data layer was clean did the BI layer get restructured for real-time reporting. Only after the BI layer was reliable did the closed-loop trend system get built on top. Each layer existed because the layer beneath it had a definition of success and a way to tell whether it was achieving it.

Five teams stood up over the same arc: merchandising, research and analytics, trend R&D, product development, product engineering. 30–35 direct reports. The function exists not because the org chart says it should — it exists because each team has a defined success metric, a reporting cadence, and a measurable hand-off to the next team in the loop. The long-tail SKU lifecycle program — a tiered dynamic markdown system indexed to aging, historical conversion, and inventory holding cost — produced an end-of-Q4 excess inventory result of 2% actual unsold (12% total, 10% safety stock). The number existed before the program. The program closed the gap to the number.

A path forward without a definition of success is a treadmill. You can run on it for years and never know whether you are getting anywhere.

## How to Practice It

Read Andy Grove, *High Output Management*, and Will Larson, *An Elegant Puzzle* — the two cleanest books available on building functions that do not require their builder to operate them. Read Ray Dalio's *Principles* selectively; the operating principles chapter is the useful one. Read the Toyota Production System literature; the andon cord is one of the great pieces of organisational design.

Then write down a function you operate. Not the whole company — one function. List its inputs, its outputs, its success metric, its review cadence, and the team that owns it. Notice the parts that are not written down. The parts that are not written down are where the function is fragile. Build infrastructure where the function is fragile.

If you are running a team, write the definition of success first, before you write the brief. If the definition of success is not measurable, keep working on it until it is. The brief becomes much shorter after that.

## The Harder Problem

The part I have not solved is the rebuild problem. The first version of a function — built from scratch, with clear definition of success, measured from day one — is straightforward. The hard version is taking over a function that already exists, has unstated rules, has unmeasured success criteria, and has years of habit holding it in place. Refactoring a live function without dropping the load is much harder than building one from scratch. I have done both. The from-scratch builds are cleaner. The refactors are slower and harder, and I am still learning how to do them with less collateral cost to the team.
